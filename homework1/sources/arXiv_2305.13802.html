Abstract

Open-set semi-supervised object detection (OSSOD) leverages practical open-set unlabeled datasets with out-of-distribution (OOD) instances for semi-supervised object detection (SSOD). The main challenge in OSSOD is distinguishing and filtering the OOD instances (i.e., outliers) from in-distribution (ID) instances during pseudo-labeling. The only OSSOD work employs an additional offline OOD detection network trained solely with labeled data for solving this problem. However, the limited training data restricts the potential for improvement. Meanwhile, the offline strategy results in low efficiency. To alleviate these issues, this paper proposes an end-to-end online OSSOD framework that improves performance and efficiency: 1) We propose a semi-supervised outlier filtering method that more effectively filters the OOD instances by using both labeled and unlabeled data. 2) We propose a threshold-free Dual Competing OOD head that further improves the performance by suppressing the mispredictions during semi-supervised outlier filtering. 3) Our proposed method is an online end-to-end trainable OSSOD framework. Experimental results show that our method achieves state-of-the-art performance on several OSSOD benchmarks compared to existing methods. Moreover, additional experiments show that our method can be easily applied to different SSOD frameworks.

Introduction

Semi-supervised learning (SSL) significantly improves the performance of various image recognition tasks by utilizing a large amount of available unlabeled data (Sohn et al. 2020a; Berthelot et al. 2019; Sohn et al. 2020b; Tarvainen and Valpola 2017; Yu et al. 2020). Object detection performance has also greatly benefited from SSL, leading to the proposal of various semi-supervised object detection (SSOD) methods (Liu et al. 2021; Li et al. 2022b, a; Xu et al. 2021; Wang et al. 2023; Zhou et al. 2022).

However, current SSOD methods are under a strong assumption that the unlabeled and labeled data are from the same label space. This assumption is somewhat unrealistic in practical situations because the unlabeled dataset in the real world usually has an open-set problem, meaning they contain OOD samples. Thus, object detectors in current SSOD methods can mistakenly classify OOD samples as ID classes. And this error can accumulate throughout the semi-supervised learning process and ultimately degrade the performance of SSOD methods.

Some works (Yu et al. 2020; Saito, Kim, and Saenko 2021) have been proposed to solve the open-set semi-supervised problem in the image classification task. However, these methods are difficult to be applied to object detection tasks directly since image classification is an image-level task, but object detection is a more challenging instance-level task. The paper of (Liu et al. 2022) is the first to tackle the OSSOD problem. They tried to directly apply existing OOD detection methods but found that their performance was not satisfactory. They further proposed the first OSSOD method by training a separate OOD detection network with labeled data to distinguish and filter out OOD instances for the SSOD framework during pseudo-labeling.

Although they have improved the performance on open-set unlabeled data, there are still some challenges that need to be addressed. Firstly, they only use labeled data to train the OOD detection network. However, in the OSSOD task, real OOD instances only exist in unlabeled data. The lack of real OOD instances results in suboptimal performance, as shown in the bottom-left of Fig. 1. Secondly, they need a manual threshold for the OOD detection network to filter out OOD instances. It is time-consuming to search for the best threshold for each dataset. Finally, their OOD detection network needs to be trained separately from the object detector and requires an additional backbone, which is inefficient considering the training process and network complexity.

To address the above issues, we propose a novel OSSOD method: Firstly, we propose a semi-supervised outlier filtering method to improve OOD filtering ability by leveraging both labeled and unlabeled data. However, we find this introduces the semantic expansion (Liu et al. 2022) problem: the mispredictions in pseudo-labels accumulate during training. As shown in the bottom-middle of Fig. 1, once the OOD instances are mispredicted (the blue cats), the decision boundary expands to misclassify more OOD labels during semi-supervised learning. To tackle this, we propose the Dual Competing OOD (DCO) head, which mitigates this issue and further improves the performance. The DCO head consists of two sub-heads that form a competitive relationship during semi-supervised outlier filtering to prevent semantic expansion, as shown in the bottom-right of Fig. 1. Moreover, the DCO head does not require any manual threshold for filtring OOD instances. Finally, we render the entire OSSOD framework end-to-end trainable.

The experimental results on several benchmarks show that our method can achieve state-of-the-art OSSOD performance. We also show that our method can be easily applied to other SSOD frameworks. In summary, this paper presents the following contributions:

  • •

    We propose a semi-supervised outlier filtering method and the threshold-free Dual Competing OOD head, which improve the OSSOD accuracy by mining more valuable instances in unlabeled data and preventing the semantic expansion problem.

  • •

    We make the entire OSSOD framework online end-to-end trainable with our proposed method to improve efficiency.

  • •

    Our proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on several OSSOD benchmarks and can be applied to other SSOD frameworks. With our method, more practical open-set unlabeled data can be used to improve the performance of object detectors.

Related works

Semi-supervised object detection.

Semi-Supervised Object Detection (SSOD) methods aim to improve object detection performance with unlabeled data. Some basic SSOD technologies are transferred from semi-supervised image classification tasks such as data augmentation (Berthelot et al. 2019), teacher-student framework (Sohn et al. 2020b), and exponential moving average (EMA) (Tarvainen and Valpola 2017). Recent SSOD research addresses unique object detection problems, such as class-wise imbalance (Liu et al. 2021), localization reliability (Li et al. 2022c; Xu et al. 2021; Li et al. 2022a), dynamic pseudo-label thresholding (Wang et al. 2023), and using dense learnable regions over hard pseudo-labels (Zhou et al. 2022). However, these methods neglect the presence of OOD instances in open-set unlabeled data. It has been shown that unfiltered pseudo labels with high confidence scores can lead to the semantic expansion problem (Liu et al. 2022).

Out-of-distribution detection.

Out-of-distribution detection methods aim to identify samples outside the target domain. Most of the OOD detection methods focus on image classification tasks. Several approaches employ different scoring methods to distinguish OOD samples, such as the Maximum Softmax Probability (Hendrycks and Gimpel 2016), Mahalanobis distance (Lee et al. 2018), and energy score (Liu et al. 2020). Others design score-enhancing methods to separate the score distribution between ID and OOD samples (Liang and Li 2017; Sun, Guo, and Li 2021; Hendrycks, Mazeika, and Dietterich 2018), or use additional structures to classify ID and OOD samples (Saito and Saenko 2021). A few OOD detection methods are tailored for object detection tasks (Du et al. 2022b, a). These OOD detection methods typically require a large amount of labeled data. However, the labeled data is scarce in the OSSOD tasks. Thus, directly applying these methods leads to lower performance, as shown in our experimental results and mentioned by (Liu et al. 2022).

Open-set object detection

The open-set object detection (OSOD) task focuses on detecting both ID and unknown OOD objects. Early approaches use dropout sampling (Miller et al. 2018) to reduce open-set errors. OWOD (Joseph et al. 2021) utilizes the energy score to discern known and unknown classes. OpenDet (Han et al. 2022) separates ID and OOD samples by identifying high/low-density regions in the latent space. The OSOD task is different from the OSSOD task in that OSOD seeks to enhance the accuracy of both ID and OOD classes, while OSSOD focuses on the performance of ID classes and prevents the detrimental effects caused by distracting OOD objects. Meanwhile, these methods also rely on substantial labeled data for training.

Open-set semi-supervised learning.

Most existing open-set semi-supervised learning (OSSSL) methods focus on image classification tasks. MTC (Yu et al. 2020) proposed a multi-task curriculum learning method to select ID samples from unlabeled data. OpenMatch (Saito, Kim, and Saenko 2021) employs a one-vs-all OOD detection method to filter OOD samples after pseudo-labeling and uses a consistency regularization loss to enhance outlier detection representations. These methods are not directly applicable to object detection tasks, where each image may contain multiple objects, and the number of detected objects can vary during training. The paper of (Liu et al. 2022) proposed the only existing OSSOD method, filtering outliers with a pre-trained OOD detection network. However, the OOD detection network is trained separately and solely with labeled data. In this paper, we improve both the accuracy and efficiency of the OSSOD approach.

Method

In this paper, we apply the Unbiased Teacher (Liu et al. 2021) following the SSOD framework in the prior section as our baseline. Fig. 2 shows the entire structure of our framework: Our proposed Dual Competing OOD (DCO) head is added to the object detector to filter the OOD instances in the pseudo-labels for SSOD. The DCO head is trained with both labeled and unlabeled data using our semi-supervised outlier filtering method.

Semi-supervised outlier filtering

The previous method (Liu et al. 2022) trains the OOD detection network with labeled data only. However, the real OOD instances only exist in the unlabeled data in the OSSOD setting. Thus, we aim to further utilize the unlabeled data to improve the OOD detection performance. To achieve this, we introduce an OOD detection head into the object detector to filtering OOD instances, which takes the feature of proposals after ROI-pooling as input and predicts the probability of each sample belonging to ID or OOD classes. We train the OOD head with both labeled and unlabeled data in a semi-supervised way.

Training on labeled data. Since the labeled data provide reliable supervision, our OOD detection head also relies on training with the annotations from labeled data. Following (Liu et al. 2022), we use the ground truth proposals as ID instances and those proposals with low overlap to the ground truth as OOD instances to train the OOD detection head.

Training on unlabeled data. When training on unlabeled data, we first get the pseudo-labeled instances from the original detection heads and label them as ID or OOD regarding the prediction of our OOD detection head. We then use these instances to train the student’s OOD detection head. It is worth noting that we can get real OOD instances and more ID instances from unlabeled data to train the head in this way. Thus, the OOD head can be exposed to a broader range of distribution characteristics present in the unlabeled data, thereby improving performance. The parameters of the OOD detection head are updated using the EMA during SSOD, so our method also benefits from the stable ID and OOD predictions from the teacher model’s OOD detection head. To make the training stable, we also sample background proposals from unlabeled data to maintain a fixed batch size.

Dual Competing OOD head

We notice that two problems arise during semi-supervised outlier filtering with previous OOD detection heads: 1) As shown in Fig. 3, The OOD detection head will inevitably generate incorrect predictions, such as labeling OOD instances as ID instances. If such pseudo-labels are used for semi-supervised outlier filtering, the student model will gradually accumulate more errors, which is named as semantic expansion problem (Liu et al. 2022). 2) A threshold for distinguishing ID and OOD instances is needed. And it is time-consuming to find the proper threshold for different datasets. To solve these problems, we propose the DCO head to further improve the performance.

Analysis of semantic expansion problem. Applying previous OOD detection heads will cause the semantic expansion problem because there is no mechanism to recheck whether a prediction is correct once it has a confidence score above the threshold. Therefore, we aim to add an additional module to constrain the original OOD detection head during the entire training process as shown in Fig. 3.

Experiments

Datasets and metrics

Our method is evaluated on the COCO-Open and COCO-OpenImages dataset proposed by the pioneering work of OSSOD (Liu et al. 2022). We also evaluate the performance on the COCO-VOC dataset.

COCO-Open. We randomly select 20/40/60 classes as ID classes and the remaining as OOD classes in the MS-COCO 2017 (Lin et al. 2014) dataset with 80 classes. The training set is divided into ID, MIX, and OOD sets by splitting the classes. The images in the ID set contain only instances of ID classes. The images in the OOD set contain only instances of OOD classes. And the images in the MIX set contain both instances of ID and OOD classes. We then randomly sample images with annotations from the ID set as the labeled dataset. The rest of the ID set and other sets are combined as the open-set unlabeled dataset. For evaluation, we use all the images in the MS-COCO 2017 validation set but delete the annotations of OOD instances.

COCO-OpenImages. We also evaluate our method on a large-scale dataset, using the entire MS-COCO 2017 as the labeled ID dataset and OpenImagesv5 (Krasin et al. 2017) as the open-set unlabeled dataset. OpenImagesv5 contains 1.7M images with 601 classes. Classes not present in MS-COCO are considered as OOD classes. For evaluation, we use the entire MS-COCO 2017 validation set.

COCO-VOC. The Pascal-VOC 2012 dataset (Everingham et al. 2010) consists of 20 classes, all of which fall within the 80 classes of the COCO dataset. We employ the Pascal VOC training set as our labeled data and the MS-COCO training set as our unlabeled data. For evaluation, we use both the MS-COCO and Pascal-VOC validation sets.

Evaluation metrics. We use the mean Average Precision (mAP) to evaluate the object detection performance and the area under the ROC curve (AUROC) to evaluate the OOD detection performance. To calculate AUROC for object detection, we label all detection results as either ID or OOD classes, depending on whether their IoU score with the annotations (containing only ID instances) exceeds 0.5.

Baseline methods

We mainly compare our method with the work (Liu et al. 2022), the only previous work tackling the OSSOD problem (referred to as offline OSSOD for convenience). The work (Liu et al. 2022) is based on the Unbiased Teacher (UT) SSOD framework (Liu et al. 2021). We also compare our method with some OOD detection methods, including OE (Hendrycks, Mazeika, and Dietterich 2018), Energy (Liu et al. 2020), OVA-Net (Saito and Saenko 2021), and VOS (Du et al. 2022b). We also compare our method with OpenDet (Han et al. 2022), which is related to the open-set object detection task.

Implementation details

For a fair comparison with previous work, we mainly use UT (Liu et al. 2021) as the basic SSOD framework, which uses Faster R-CNN (Ren et al. 2015) with Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) (Lin et al. 2017) and ResNet-50 (He et al. 2016) backbone. We keep the same hyper-parameter settings with UT and offline OSSOD, including the learning rate, SSOD thresholds, training schedule, etc. The only new hyper-parameter of our work is the weight λo​o​dsuperscript𝜆𝑜𝑜𝑑\lambda^{ood} of the OOD detection loss ℒD​C​Osuperscriptℒ𝐷𝐶𝑂\mathcal{L}^{DCO}. We set it to 0.1. The other hyper-parameters will be reported in detail in the appendix. The whole framework is based on Detectron2 (Wu et al. 2019).

The results in Fig. 4 show that 1) OOD instances are harmful to SSOD, and OSSOD methods can alleviate the problem. 2) Our method is better than offline OSSOD among all the combinations.

The results in Tab. 1 and Tab. 2 show that our method consistently outperforms offline OSSOD across these scenarios. Details of the selected ID classes can be found in the appendix. Comparsion on the large-scale dataset. Moreover, we also show the effectiveness of our method on the large-scale data combination of MS-COCO and OpenImagesv5. Ablation studies of our method

Ablation study of semi-supervised outlier filtering. We show the benefit of mining more instances from unlabeled data. Evidence of this is seen in Tab. 4, where the performance of our positive head trained with labeled data only (23.86 mAP) is compared with that trained using both labeled and unlabeled data (25.01 mAP). In this case, we only use the positive head because it is equivalent to the negative head when using only labeled data. Note that the positive head is actually the same structure as the head in offline OSSOD but without the additional backbone. We also apply the same OOD score and threshold with offline OSSOD when using positive head only. We also find that applying previous methods results in relatively lower performance, which aligns with the conclusions drawn in offline OSSOD. This may be because these methods are designed to be trained with abundant labeled data, thus, they are not suitable for the OSSOD tasks with limited labeled data. Meanwhile, these methods, except OpenDet, need a threshold when calculating the mAP metric. We either utilize provided values or employ their value-finding methods to set the threshold. we also analyze that a higher AUROC does not always ensure a better detection performance, as undetected ID objects(false negative) are not reflected in its value. As a result, using the Energy score gains only 21.00 mAP with 79.47 AUROC, since most of its detection results are false positives with high OOD confidence scores.

Effectiveness of the DCO head. While our method outperforms the previous method with only the positive head using semi-supervised outlier filtering, we find that incorporating our proposed DCO head can further enhance performance. As shown in the last three rows in Tab. 4, applying the entire DCO head with both positive and negative heads, yields the best performance among all tested methods. We also observe that solely using the negative head results in reduced accuracy during the later stages of training.

Further analysis of the DCO head. We further analyze the effectiveness of our DCO head by monitoring the number of ID and OOD pseudo-labels during training. We sample 1000 images from the unlabeled set in COCO-Open with their ID label annotations (the annotations are not used during our training). Pseudo-labels having an IoU score over 0.5 with the ID annotations are considered as ID boxes, otherwise OOD boxes. As shown in Fig. 5, compared with the positive or negative head only, our DCO head will gradually generate fewer OOD boxes during training but keep almost the same number of ID boxes. This occurs as the negative head gradually identifies OOD instances with increasing confidence throughout the training process. This phenomenon matches the purpose for which we designed the DCO head, thus confirming its effectiveness. This experiment is conducted on COCO-Open with 40 ID classes and 4000 labeled images.

Additional experiments.

More SSOD framework and open-set dataset. We apply our method to another SSOD framework, SoftTeacher (Xu et al. 2021). The results in Table 5 show that our method can improve its performance on the OSSOD task by 1.0 mAP.

More open-set datasets. Additionally, we evaluate our method on another open-set dataset combination: VOC-COCO. The results in Tab. 6 show that our method can also improve the detection performance on this benchmark. Note that the Pascal-VOC validation set contains ID instances only, and the MS-COCO validation set contains both ID and OOD instances. Thus, the effectiveness of our method is more significant on the MS-COCO validation set.

Efficiency of our method. Offline OSSOD needs three-step training: Firstly, train an object detector with labeled data only. Then, train an additional OOD detection network using the proposals from the pre-trained detector. Finally, train the SSOD framework with the frozen OOD detection network. Our method only needs the third step to train the SSOD framework and can converge within the same training iteration. The additional DCO head only consists of two classification heads, resulting in just a 1% increase in training time. And this head can be removed after training. Therefore, our method is more efficient.

Visulization results. We show some visualization results of ID and OOD pseudo-labels during training in Fig. 6. More visualization results are available in the appendix.

Conclusions

In this paper, we proposed an online end-to-end trainable open-set semi-supervised object detection framework with semi-supervised outlier filtering and the Dual Competing OOD head to solve the problem of SSOD on open-set unlabeled data. Experimental results on several benchmarks demonstrate that the proposed method achieves better performance compared with the state-of-the-art methods. We also conducted ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of each component of our method. And we further show the flexibility of our methods on other SSOD frameworks and open-set datasets. With our proposed method, when given an object detection task, in addition to training with precious labeled data, we can leverage more existing unlabeled data to improve the performance of the model without the need for any additional manual labeling and filtering. Therefore, our method has a high practical value, particularly when the target task has a limited amount of close-set data available.
