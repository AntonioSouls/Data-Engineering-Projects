Improving Online Source-free Domain Adaptation for Object Detection by Unsupervised Data Acquisition

Xiangyu Shi, Yanyuan Qiao, Qi Wu, Lingqiao Liu and Feras Dayoub The authors are with the School of Computer Science and the Australian Institute for Machine Learning at the University of Adelaide, Adelaide, Australia (xiangyu.shi@adelaide.edu.au).
Abstract

Effective object detection in mobile robots is challenged by deployment in diverse and unfamiliar environments. Online Source-Free Domain Adaptation (O-SFDA) offers real-time model adaptation using a stream of unlabeled data from a target domain. However, not all captured frames in mobile robotics contain information that is beneficial for adaptation, particularly when there is a strong domain shift. This paper introduces a novel approach to enhance O-SFDA for adaptive object detection in mobile robots via unsupervised data acquisition. Our methodology prioritizes the most informative unlabeled samples for inclusion in the online training process. Empirical evaluation on a real-world dataset reveals that our method outperforms existing state-of-the-art O-SFDA techniques, demonstrating the viability of unsupervised data acquisition for improving adaptive object detection in mobile robots.

I INTRODUCTION

Mobile robots operating in diverse and ever-changing real-world environments require robust object detection systems that can adapt to new surroundings. However, the object detection models that power these mobile robots are typically trained on large datasets that are ideally representative of all possible deployment environments. In fact, the real world is seldom so accommodating; the environments encountered may vary widely in their characteristics, thereby presenting a domain shift that can severely compromise the performance of a pre-trained object detection model.

The challenge lies in equipping mobile robots with the ability to adapt to new domains “on-the-fly,” without the need for manually labeling new data or undergoing an exhaustive re-training process. Online source-free domain adaptation (O-SFDA) [1, 2] offers a promising avenue to address this challenge by transferring knowledge from a source domain to a target domain in an online manner. This means that the adaptation occurs concurrently with the deployment in the target domain. This contrasts with traditional methods, which may require source data batch or offline processing [3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8].

Current O-SFDA for object detection methods [2] offer a path towards domain adaptation but are limited when it comes to mobile robotics. These methods may suffer from high computational costs, the inability to identify informative unlabeled data, or an overreliance on data that does not provide unique adaptation-worthy information. Such shortcomings often lead to subpar object detection performance.

To address these challenges, we employ an unsupervised data acquisition strategy based on incremental online clustering to dynamically identify and integrate the most informative unlabeled frames into the learning process. We augment this strategy within the optimization of a dual architecture consisting of a teacher model and a student model based on the Mean Teacher framework [9]. Our method is designed to reduce the use of computational resources while enhancing object detection adaptivity in previously unencountered deployment environments. Fig. 1 shows the difference between O-SFDA with/without data acquisition.

Our comprehensive experiments span multiple datasets, including real-world scenarios with varying complexities. The results demonstrate that our method consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art O-SFDA techniques in object detection.

In summary, the contribution of this paper is the introduction of a novel approach to improve O-SFDA for object detection on mobile robots via an unsupervised data acquisition methodology based on incremental online clustering. Our method dynamically identifies and includes the most informative unlabeled frames for online training. This tackles the issue of unnecessary or less informative data interfering with the training process, which has been a limitation in previous O-SFDA approaches for object detection.

II RELATED WORK

II-A Unsupervised Domain Adaptation for Object Detection

Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) aims to mitigate the domain-shift problem without requiring labeled target data, by aligning source and target distributions. The field has seen significant contributions specifically in object detection [3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 10, 11]. For instance, Chen et al. [3] pioneered this space by proposing domain adaptation via both image-level and instance-level alignments. Later, Zhu et al. [12] focused on the alignment of congruent regions between source and target domains. Xu et al. [13] presented a framework that involves Categorical Regularization at the Image-Level and Consistency Regularization for Categories to address complex challenges in this domain. While these approaches have yielded impressive results, they inherently rely on the availability of source data during adaptation, limiting their applicability in real-world scenarios such as robotics where source data may not always be accessible.

II-B Online Source-free Domain Adaptation for Object Detection

In environments where source data is not readily available, such as in autonomous vehicles, the specialized domain of Source-Free Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (SFDA) has garnered interest. SFDA uniquely operates without the source data during the adaptation phase, typically focusing on offline model adaptation [2, 9, 14, 15, 16]. The significance of online adaptation in such settings cannot be overstated. Vibashan et al. [2] ventured into this area by proposing a cross-attention transformer-based model for both online and offline SFDA in object detection. They extended the Mean Teacher framework with a MemXformer that pairs features from the teacher and student models [9]. Despite these advancements, existing methods have not addressed the importance of keyframe selection for adaptation.

Our work fills this gap by emphasizing online adaptation in SFDA for object detection, with a particular focus on selective adaptation through keyframe selection. This not only makes the adaptation process more efficient and suited for real-time applications but also provides superior adaptation performance. Notably, Colomer et al. [17] consider frame selection by evaluating the domain gap in semantic segmentation; however, their approach is not source-free.

We also introduce our data acquisition strategy by incremental online clustering and detail our model optimization approach. III-D Model Optimization

For the optimization of the teacher model and student model, we first use the teacher’s pseudo label to supervise the student model for parameter update. After the student model is updated, we do EMA to update the teacher model, which transfers the knowledge from the student model to the teacher model.

Specifically, to update the student model, we first apply strong data augmentation to produce ftssuperscriptsubscript𝑓𝑡𝑠f_{t}^{s} upon identifying a current frame ftsubscript𝑓𝑡f_{t} as a key frame. This augmented frame is then processed by the student model to predict labels ySsubscript𝑦Sy_{\text{S}}. Given the absence of true labels in the target domain, we employ pseudo-labels y^Tsubscript^𝑦T\hat{y}_{\text{T}} generated by the teacher model to adapt the student model parameters ΘSsubscriptΘS\Theta_{\text{S}}. IV Experiment Setting

IV-A Datasets

We evaluate our model using four rigorously selected datasets: Cityscapes [19], Cityscapes-Foggy [20], Sim10k [21], and SHIFT [22]. Each dataset was chosen to scrutinize different aspects of our approach.

  • •

    Cityscapes: Geared towards urban street environments, Cityscapes consists of 2,975 training images and 500 validation images. An additional sequence of 106,917 images from Frankfurt allows for comprehensive evaluations in online sequential adaptation scenarios. We excluded the labelled 267 frames from the Frankfurt sequence to evaluate our model’s performance in the target domain. The performance on these 267 frames is reported as “mAP Frankfurt” in the results section.

  • •

    Cityscapes-Foggy: This dataset augments the original Cityscapes set with simulated fog, making it an excellent benchmark for evaluating adaptation to adverse weather conditions.

  • •

    Sim10k: Originating from the video game Grand Theft Auto V, this dataset comprises 10,000 training frames and tests our model’s ability to adapt to substantially different source domains.

  • •

    SHIFT: Created in a simulator, SHIFT encompasses a variety of weather conditions, providing an ideal landscape for assessing the robustness of our model across diverse environments.

IV-B Evaluation Scenarios

Our approach is evaluated under two distinct, yet interrelated, scenarios:

Online Sequential Adaptation: Our primary objective is real-time adaptation for mobile robots navigating through sequences of overlapping frames. We employ datasets Cityscapes and Sim10k to assess this feature. Detailed evaluation setups include:

  1. 1.

    Sim10k to Seq-Cityscapes with car category only: Utilizing Sim10k as the source domain, we evaluate our model’s adaptability using the sequential Frankfurt set of the Cityscapes dataset.

  2. 2.

    SHIFT to Seq-Cityscapes with four categories: In this experiment, SHIFT serves as the source domain to evaluate the model on the sequential Cityscapes dataset.

Weather Adaptation: This scenario scrutinizes the model’s resilience to sudden environmental changes, particularly shifts in weather conditions. Cityscapes and Cityscapes-Foggy serve as key evaluation grounds. In addition, we introduce an extra evaluation set from the Cityscapes validation set to assess the model’s robustness in unseen urban settings.

IV-C Baseline Methods for Comparison

We evaluate our approach against two baseline methods, one of which is the current state-of-the-art in O-SFDA for object detection:

  1. 1.

    MemCLR [2]: This transformer-based student-teacher method specializes in learning Region of Interest (RoI) features from a teacher model.

  2. 2.

    TENT [23]: TENT employs entropy minimization to boost model performance in classification and semantic segmentation tasks.

It is noteworthy that neither of these baseline methods incorporates mechanisms for selecting keyframes for adaptation, a critical feature addressed in our proposed method.

IV-D Implementation Details

Our implementation builds upon the Faster-RCNN framework [18] with a ResNet50 backbone [24], following the baseline established by MemCLR [2]. The ResNet50 model is pre-trained on ImageNet [25].

Operating within the O-SFDA framework, the experiment settings are as follows:

  • •

    Batch Size and Epochs: We employ a batch size of 1 and run the experiment for 1 epoch to ensure real-time adaptation capabilities.

  • •

    Pseudo Labeling: The student model relies heavily on the teacher’s predictions for adaptation. To guarantee robustness, we only consider teacher-generated pseudo labels with a confidence score exceeding 0.9 as Ground Truth for guiding the student model.

  • •

    EMA Parameter: The Exponential Moving Average (EMA) parameter is initially set to 0.996 during the adaptation phase and adjusted to 0.9 post-adaptation to ensure stable learning.

  • •

    Learning Rate and Optimizer: The learning rate is set to 0.001, and optimization is performed using the SDG optimizer.

Evaluation Metric: We assess model performance using the mean Average Precision (mAP) calculated via the AP50 metric. The Intersection over Union (IoU) threshold for this evaluation is set at 0.5.

VI DISCUSSION

We explored a technique designed to maximize performance by enabling the model to randomly select 400 frames on five separate trails. The striking effectiveness of this random 400-frame selection can be attributed to its ability to facilitate optimal distribution adaptation within the model. Bengar et al. [27] similarly found that random sampling exhibits competitive performance when the selected frame count is less than 1%percent11\% of the total.

In the unique setting of online training, characterized by a batch size and epoch count of just one and a relatively modest evaluation set, random seed selection for augmentation becomes a pivotal variable. Different seed values guide the model toward diverse performance outcomes. To ensure the robustness of our results, we conducted each experiment five times using random seeds, and subsequently calculated the mean and standard deviation to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the model’s behaviour.

Our study currently concentrates on the use of a representation-based unsupervised data acquisition method, combined with different encoders, to identify the optimal frame distribution for selection and to explore how these encoders influence the results. As a next step, we plan to investigate a hybrid approach that merges representation-based and information-based clustering methods for more effective data acquisition.

VII CONCLUSION

In this work, we have tackled the challenge of real-time adaptive object detection for mobile robots operating in diverse and dynamic environments. Our novel approach centres on enhancing O-SFDA object detection through an unsupervised data acquisition method. Empirical evaluations across multiple datasets affirm the efficacy of our approach, outperforming existing methods in terms of object detection performance. While our method demonstrates robust performance, future work could explore additional online clustering techniques.
