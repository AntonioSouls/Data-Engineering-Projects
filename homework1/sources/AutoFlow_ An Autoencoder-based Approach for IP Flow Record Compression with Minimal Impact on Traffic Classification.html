AutoFlow: An Autoencoder-based Approach for IP Flow Record Compression with Minimal Impact on Traffic Classification

Adrian Pekar Department of Networked Systems and Services, Faculty of Electrical Engineering and Informatics,
Budapest University of Technology and Economics, Műegyetem rkp. 3., H-1111 Budapest, Hungary.
HUN-REN-BME Information Systems Research Group, Magyar Tudósok krt. 2, 1117 Budapest, Hungary.
Email: apekar@hit.bme.hu
Abstract

This paper presents a novel approach to compressing IP flow records using deep learning techniques, specifically autoencoders. Our method aims to significantly reduce data volume while maintaining the utility of the compressed data for downstream analysis tasks. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach through extensive experiments on a large-scale, real-world network traffic dataset. The proposed autoencoder-based compression achieves a 3.28x reduction in data size while preserving 99.20% accuracy in a multi-class traffic classification task, compared to 99.77% accuracy with uncompressed data. This marginal decrease in performance is offset by substantial gains in storage efficiency and potential improvements in processing speed. Our method shows particular promise in distinguishing between various modern application protocols, including encrypted traffic from popular services. The implications of this work extend to more efficient network monitoring, real-time analysis in resource-constrained environments, and scalable network management solutions.

Index Terms:
Network Traffic Analysis, IP Flow Compression, Deep Learning, Autoencoders, Traffic Classification

I Introduction

Network traffic analysis and classification play crucial roles in modern network management, security, and quality of service provisioning. As network infrastructures continue to grow in complexity and scale, the volume of data generated by traffic monitoring systems has increased exponentially [cisco2020annual]. This data deluge poses significant challenges for storage, processing, and real-time analysis of network traffic information.

IP flow records, which provide aggregated information about network connections, are widely used for traffic analysis tasks [Hofstede2014, Sadre2014]. However, the sheer volume of these records, especially in large-scale networks, can overwhelm traditional storage and processing systems. This challenge is further exacerbated by the need for rapid, often real-time, analysis for tasks such as anomaly detection, traffic engineering, and application performance monitoring [Boutaba2018].

To address these challenges, there is a pressing need for efficient data compression techniques that can significantly reduce the storage footprint of IP flow records while preserving their utility for downstream analysis tasks. Traditional compression methods often fail to capture the inherent structure and relationships within network traffic data, potentially leading to loss of critical information for analysis [Tongaonkar2015].

In this paper, we propose a novel approach to IP flow record compression using deep learning techniques, specifically autoencoders. Our method leverages the power of neural networks to learn compact, low-dimensional representations of IP flow records that retain the essential characteristics needed for accurate traffic classification. By doing so, we aim to achieve a significant reduction in data volume while maintaining high performance in downstream analysis tasks.

Our experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach, achieving a compression ratio of 3.28x while maintaining a remarkable 99.20% accuracy in a multi-class traffic classification task. This represents only a marginal decrease from the 99.77% accuracy achieved with uncompressed data, highlighting the method’s ability to preserve critical information. The proposed technique shows particular promise in distinguishing between various modern application protocols, including encrypted traffic from popular services like Facebook, TikTok, and WhatsApp.

The implications of this work are far-reaching, potentially enabling more efficient storage and processing of network monitoring data, facilitating real-time analysis in resource-constrained environments, and paving the way for more scalable network management solutions. Furthermore, the compressed representations learned by our model may offer new insights into the underlying structure of network traffic, potentially leading to improved analysis techniques.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section II discusses related work relevant in the context of this work. Section III details our proposed autoencoder-based compression method. Section IV presents and analyzes our results. Section V discusses the results, implications, applicability, and future research directions. Section VI concludes the paper.

II Related Work

Data compression in network traffic has been extensively studied to improve bandwidth utilization, reduce storage requirements, and enhance processing efficiency.

Header compression techniques aim to reduce overhead for efficient bandwidth usage. For example, \citeauthorFarouq2020 [Farouq2020] proposed methods to compress headers in real-time video streaming over UDP/IP and HTTP/TCP flows, achieving header size reductions of up to 90% for RTP/UDP/IP. \citeauthorWestphal2003-1 [Westphal2003-1, Westphal2003-2] developed schemes exploiting similarities in consecutive packet headers to enhance bandwidth utilization in wireless networks by combining time and space compression algorithms.

\citeauthor

Sonai2024WSN [Sonai2024WSN] introduced a statistical compression method for wireless sensor networks, achieving a 91.23% memory reduction and 79.65% energy savings through normalization and encoding/decoding algorithms. \citeauthorSonai2024CTLA [Sonai2024CTLA] also developed the Compressed Table Look-up Algorithm for OpenFlow switches, which combines Huffman encoding with a modified trie structure, hashing, and recursive binary search to optimize IPv4 and IPv6 lookups, achieving a 37% and 61% space reduction, respectively, and improving lookup time complexity [Sonai2024CTLA].

Packet-level compression has also been investigated. \citeauthorChen2008 [Chen2008] introduced IPzip, a suite of algorithms that compress IP packet headers and payloads by exploiting inter-packet and intra-packet correlations. \citeauthorHuang2014 [Huang2014] proposed memory-assisted compression techniques using clustering algorithms, achieving nearly 70% traffic reduction on real Internet data.

While these studies offer valuable insights into network data compression, they focus on individual packet headers, payloads, or flow tables within network devices. To the best of our knowledge, there is a lack of research addressing the compression of IP flow records—aggregated summaries of network traffic essential for monitoring and analysis.

Our work addresses this gap by proposing an autoencoder-based method for compressing IP flow records. By leveraging deep learning techniques, specifically autoencoders, we learn compact representations of flow records that preserve essential characteristics necessary for accurate traffic classification. This approach differs from prior methods by focusing on flow records and utilizing deep learning for compression, enabling significant data reduction without compromising utility in downstream analysis tasks.

This novel approach addresses the challenges posed by the increasing volume of flow data in modern networks. By achieving significant data reduction while maintaining high classification accuracy, our method provides a foundation for more scalable network monitoring and analysis solutions.

III Methodology

This section details our approach to IP flow record compression and subsequent traffic classification. We present a novel method that leverages autoencoders to create compact representations of IP flow records while preserving their utility for traffic classification tasks.

III-A Dataset Description

The dataset utilized in this study comprises 3 163 14031631403\,163\,1403 163 140 network flows, measured in a university dormitory network, providing a robust foundation for our methodology verification [Pekar2024]. It encompasses a diverse set of 91 flow features [nfs-core-feat], including volume and count characteristics, statistical summaries (minimum, mean, maximum, and standard deviation) of packet lengths and inter-arrival times, application layer characteristics, as well as properties that serve as the flow key. The dataset represents a wide range of application types: Web traffic (HTTP, HTTPS), streaming services (YouTube), social media platforms (Facebook, TikTok, Instagram), messaging applications (WhatsApp, Discord), file transfer protocols (BitTorrent), and remote desktop protocols (RDP).

III-B Feature Selection for Compression

Not all features are equally suitable for compression. The impact of erroneous reconstruction varies based on the feature. For instance, reconstructing a flow size of 225 MB as 222 MB may be acceptable in some contexts, whereas reconstructing a timestamp incorrectly can lead to major inaccuracies in event sequencing and analysis. Features not suitable for compression include:

  • •

    Timestamps: Features like flow start and flow end times, which are given in epochs, require high accuracy. Erroneous reconstruction of these features can lead to substantial errors in temporal analysis.

  • •

    IP Addresses: Source and destination IP addresses are critical for identifying endpoints and cannot be compressed without losing essential information.

  • •

    Port Numbers: Source and destination port numbers are crucial for identifying specific services and applications. An incorrect reconstruction (e.g., reconstructing port 22 as port 12) would result in significant misinterpretation of the network traffic.

  • •

    Protocol: The protocol used for the flow (e.g., TCP, UDP) must be accurately preserved to ensure correct interpretation of the data.

We selected features for compression based on the potential impact of reconstruction errors:

  • •

    Bidirectional metrics: Duration in milliseconds, packet counts, byte counts.

  • •

    Source to destination metrics: Duration in milliseconds, packet counts, byte counts.

  • •

    Destination to source metrics: Duration in milliseconds, packet counts, byte counts.

  • •

    Statistical packet size metrics: Minimum, mean, standard deviation, and maximum packet sizes for both bidirectional and directional flows.

This selection results in a total of 21 features suitable for compression. These features were chosen to ensure that our compression focuses on attributes where slight inaccuracies in reconstruction are less likely to significantly impact subsequent analysis, while still providing a comprehensive representation of the network flows.

III-H Traffic Classification

To evaluate the utility of our compressed representations for downstream tasks, we perform traffic classification using both the original and compressed features:

  1. 1.

    Feature Extraction: For the compressed version, we use the encoder part of the trained autoencoder to generate the 16-dimensional compressed representations of the IP flow records.

  2. 2.

    Classifier: We use a Random Forest classifier with 100 estimators for both the original and compressed features. Random Forest is chosen for its robustness, ability to handle non-linear relationships, and resistance to overfitting [Breiman2001].

  3. 3.

    Training and Evaluation: We split the data into training (80%) and test (20%) sets, stratified by the target variable to ensure representative class distributions in both sets.

  4. 4.

    Performance Metrics: We assess the classification performance using:

    • •

      Accuracy: The proportion of correct predictions among the total number of cases examined.

    • •

      Precision, Recall, and F1-score: Computed for each class and averaged (macro and weighted averages).

    • •

      Normalized Confusion Matrices: To visualize the performance across different classes.

  5. 5.

    Comparative Analysis: We compare the classification performance between the original and compressed features to assess the impact of our compression method on the downstream task.

  6. 6.

    Misclassification Analysis: We perform a detailed analysis of misclassifications to understand the strengths and limitations of our compressed representations compared to the original features.

This comprehensive evaluation allows us to assess not only the effectiveness of our compression method but also its practical utility in the critical task of network traffic classification.

IV Results

This section presents the results of our experiments, evaluating both the compression performance of our autoencoder model and its impact on the downstream task of traffic classification.

IV-A Autoencoder Performance

Fig. 1 illustrates the training and test loss progression over 200 epochs, revealing several noteworthy characteristics of the learning process. The loss curves show a sharp decrease in both training and test losses during the first 25 epochs, indicating efficient learning of the data’s primary structures. Following this initial drop, the losses stabilize with only minor fluctuations, suggesting a robust learning process. Notably, the training and test losses closely follow each other throughout, indicating good generalization and absence of overfitting. Table I summarizes the key performance metrics of our autoencoder model. The compression ratio of 3.28 indicates that our model successfully reduced the data volume to approximately 30.5% of its original size, a significant achievement in data compression.

At first glance, the MSE and RMSE values might appear high, and the MAPE value seems extraordinarily large. However, it is crucial to interpret these metrics in the context of our data. These values are calculated on the original scale of the features, which include wide-ranging values such as byte counts and durations in milliseconds. Consequently, even small percentage errors in large-magnitude features can lead to substantial absolute errors, inflating these metrics.

The extremely high MAPE value, in particular, results from features that occasionally take values very close to zero in the original data. When the original value is near zero, even a small absolute difference in the reconstruction can lead to a massive percentage error, dramatically inflating the mean across all data points. This phenomenon underscores the importance of considering multiple error metrics and understanding their limitations in the context of the specific dataset.

V Discussion

This study presents a novel approach to IP flow record compression using autoencoders, demonstrating significant data reduction while maintaining high fidelity for downstream traffic classification tasks. Our findings have several important implications for network traffic analysis and management.

V-A Interpretation of Compression Performance

The autoencoder achieved a compression ratio of 3.28, reducing the data to approximately 30.5% of its original size. This substantial reduction in data volume has significant implications for storage efficiency and computational resource management in large-scale network monitoring systems.

The compression performance, characterized by an MSE of 2,514,207.4427 and an RMSE of 1,585.6253, might initially appear to indicate poor reconstruction. However, these metrics must be interpreted in the context of the original data scale:

  • •

    Scale Sensitivity: Features like byte counts and durations can have values in the millions or billions. For instance, a flow with 1,000,000,000 bytes reconstructed as 998,500,000 bytes would be highly accurate in relative terms but contribute a large absolute error of 1,500,000 to the MSE.

  • •

    Relative Error: The median percentage errors, ranging from 0.02% to 2.32% across features, provide a more intuitive measure of reconstruction accuracy. These low percentage errors indicate excellent reconstruction for most data points.

  • •

    Distribution Preservation: The varied KL divergence values suggest that while point-wise reconstruction is generally accurate, preserving the overall distribution is more challenging for some features, particularly packet size statistics.

The error distribution (provided as digital artifact at [github]) further supports this interpretation, showing that the vast majority of reconstructions have very low percentage errors, with the peak around 0.5%.

V-B Implications for Traffic Classification

The downstream classification results demonstrate the practical utility of our compression method:

  • •

    Minimal Performance Drop: The reduction in classification accuracy from 99.77% to 99.22% represents a minimal performance drop of only 0.55 percentage points, despite a 3.28x reduction in data volume.

  • •

    Class-Specific Performance: Most traffic classes maintain near-perfect classification accuracy, with slight degradation primarily in TLS-based traffic (Facebook and TikTok).

  • •

    Misclassification Analysis: While the number of misclassifications increased from 1,446 to 4,955 with compressed features, this still represents a very small fraction (less than 0.8%) of the 632,693 test samples.

These results suggest that our compression method preserves the most critical discriminative information for traffic classification while significantly reducing data volume.

V-C Real-World Applicability

The dual capability—efficient compression for storage and transmission, coupled with high-fidelity reconstruction and excellent performance in downstream tasks—of our proposed method has several potential applications in real-world network management and security scenarios:

  • •

    Efficient Storage: Network operators can store 3.28 times more historical flow data in the same storage space, enabling longer-term trend analysis and anomaly detection.

  • •

    Faster Processing: Reduced data volume can lead to faster query and analysis times, potentially enabling more responsive network management and security systems.

  • •

    Bandwidth Conservation: In distributed network monitoring systems, compressed flow records can be transmitted between collection points and central analysis servers with lower bandwidth requirements.

  • •

    Resource-Constrained Environments: The method could be particularly valuable in edge computing or IoT scenarios where storage and processing capabilities are limited.

V-D Computational Complexity and Practical Implementation

While our method shows promising results, it is crucial to consider the computational aspects, especially for real-world implementations:

  • •

    Training Complexity: The training process of the autoencoder has a time complexity of O(NDE), where N is the number of training samples, D is the input dimension, and E is the number of epochs. This can be computationally intensive for large datasets.

  • •

    Encoding Complexity: Once trained, the encoding process (compression) has a time complexity of O(DH), where D is the input dimension and H is the size of the hidden layer. This is typically very efficient, allowing for real-time compression of incoming flow records.

  • •

    Decoding Complexity: Similarly, the decoding process has a time complexity of O(HD), which is also efficient for on-demand reconstruction of compressed data.

  • •

    Space Complexity: The space complexity for storing the model is O(DH + HD), which is typically negligible compared to the size of the dataset it compresses.

In practice, the computational intensity of the training phase might not be a significant issue for several reasons:

  1. 1.

    One-time Training: The bulk of the computational cost is in the initial training, which can be done offline using dedicated hardware.

  2. 2.

    Transfer Learning: Once a model is trained on a representative dataset, it can often be fine-tuned for specific network environments with minimal additional training.

  3. 3.

    Infrequent Retraining: In many cases, the model would only need occasional retraining to adapt to evolving traffic patterns, perhaps on a weekly or monthly basis.

  4. 4.

    Efficient Encoding/Decoding: The trained model can be deployed for efficient real-time encoding of incoming flows and on-demand decoding when analysis is required.

This approach allows for a balance between computational cost and up-to-date model performance, making it feasible for real-world applications even in large-scale environments.

V-E Limitations and Future Directions

While our results are promising, several limitations and areas for future research emerge:

  • •

    Autoencoder Architecture Variations: This study focuses on a Vanilla Autoencoder. Comparisons with a Denoising Autoencoder are available as a digital artifact [github]. Future work could explore advanced architectures like Sparse, Contractive, Adversarial, and Variational Autoencoders to potentially enhance performance.

  • •

    Hidden Layer Size: In our study, we set the hidden layer size to 16, achieving a compression ratio of 3.28. This choice represents a balance between compression and reconstruction accuracy. However, it is important to note that different layer sizes could yield different results:

    • –

      Smaller hidden layers could achieve higher compression ratios but potentially at the cost of increased reconstruction error and impact on downstream task performance.

    • –

      Larger hidden layers might improve reconstruction and downstream task performance but with diminished compression benefits.

    Future work could explore the full spectrum of this trade-off, potentially offering network operators a tunable parameter to balance storage savings with task-specific performance requirements.

  • •

    Feature Distribution Preservation: The higher KL divergence values for some features, particularly packet size statistics, suggest room for improvement in preserving complex distributions. Future work could explore more sophisticated autoencoder architectures or loss functions designed to better capture these distributions.

  • •

    TLS Traffic Differentiation: The increased confusion between TLS.Facebook and TLS.TikTok in the compressed version indicates a challenge in distinguishing encrypted traffic from similar applications. Further research could focus on identifying and preserving the subtle features that differentiate these traffic types.

  • •

    Temporal Aspects: Our current study focuses on compressing individual flow records. Future work could explore incorporating temporal relationships between flows, potentially using recurrent neural network architectures to capture time-dependent patterns in network traffic.

  • •

    Adversarial Robustness: As with many machine learning applications in security-sensitive domains, the robustness of this method against adversarial attempts to evade classification should be studied.

  • •

    Computational Overhead: While we achieve significant data reduction, the real-time performance of encoding and decoding in high-throughput network environments needs further evaluation, as discussed in Section V-D.

In conclusion, our autoencoder-based compression method for IP flow records demonstrates a promising approach to addressing the challenges of big data in network traffic analysis. By achieving significant data reduction while maintaining high classification accuracy, this method paves the way for more efficient and scalable network management and security systems. Future research addressing the identified limitations and exploring the trade-offs in hidden layer size could further enhance the applicability and effectiveness of this approach in real-world scenarios.

VI Conclusion

This paper presented a novel approach to IP flow record compression using autoencoders, demonstrating significant data reduction while maintaining high fidelity for downstream traffic classification tasks. Our method achieved a compression ratio of 3.28, reducing data volume by 69.5% while preserving critical information for accurate traffic classification. The compressed representations maintained a classification accuracy of 99.22%, compared to 99.77% with original features, across a diverse set of network applications including encrypted traffic. This minimal performance drop, coupled with substantial data reduction, addresses the growing challenges of storing and processing large volumes of network monitoring data in modern infrastructures.
