Abstract

Knowledge Distillation (KD) has been validated as an effective model compression technique for learning compact object detectors. Existing state-of-the-art KD methods for object detection are mostly based on feature imitation, which is generally observed to be better than prediction mimicking. In this paper, we show that the inconsistency of the optimization objectives between the ground-truth signals and distillation targets is the key reason for the inefficiency of prediction mimicking. To alleviate this issue, we present a simple yet effective distillation scheme, termed CrossKD, which delivers the intermediate features of the studentâ€™s detection head to the teacherâ€™s detection head. The resulting cross-head predictions are then forced to mimic the teacherâ€™s predictions. Such a distillation manner relieves the studentâ€™s head from receiving contradictory supervision signals from the ground-truth annotations and the teacherâ€™s predictions, greatly improving the studentâ€™s detection performance. On MS COCO, with only prediction mimicking losses applied, our CrossKD boosts the average precision of GFL ResNet-50 with 1Ã—\times training schedule from 40.2 to 43.7, outperforming all existing KD methods for object detection.

1 Introduction

Knowledge Distillation (KD), serving as a model compression technique, has been deeply studied in object detectionÂ [65, 3, 52, 9, 25, 64] and has received excellent performance recently. According to the distillation position of the detectors, existing KD methods can be roughly classified into two categories: prediction mimicking and feature imitation. Prediction mimicking (See \figreffig:comparison(a)) was first proposed inÂ [20], which points out that the smooth distribution of the teacherâ€™s predictions is more comfortable for the student to learn than the Dirac distribution of the ground truths. In other words, prediction mimicking forces the student to resemble the prediction distribution of the teacher. Differently, feature imitation (See \figreffig:comparison(b)) follows the idea proposed in FitNetÂ [47], which argues that intermediate features contain more information than the predictions from the teacher. It aims to enforce the feature consistency between the teacher-student pair.

Prediction mimicking plays a vital role in distilling object detection models. However, it has been observed to be more inefficient than feature imitation for a long time. Typically, Zheng et al.Â [64] proposed a localization distillation (LD) method that improves prediction mimicking by transferring localization knowledge, which pushes the prediction mimicking to a new level. Despite just catching up with the advanced feature imitation methods, e.g., PKDÂ [3], LD shows that prediction mimicking has the ability to transfer task-specific knowledge, which benefits the student from the orthogonal aspect to feature imitation. This motivates us to further explore and improve prediction mimicking.

Through observation, we find that prediction mimicking needs to confront the conflict between ground-truth targets and distillation targets, which has been neglected by previous works. When training a detector with prediction mimicking, the studentâ€™s predictions are forced to mimic both the ground-truth targets and the teacherâ€™s predictions simultaneously. However, the distillation targets predicted by the teacher usually have a large discrepancy with the ground-truth targets assigned to the student. As shown in theÂ \figreffig:problems, the teacher produces an inaccuracy class probabilities in the green circled areas, which conflicts with the ground-truth targets. As a result, the student detector experiences a contradictory learning process during distillation, which we argue is the main reason hindering prediction mimicking from achieving higher performance.

In this paper, we present a novel cross-head knowledge distillation pipeline, abbreviated as CrossKD, to alleviate the target conflict problem. As illustrated in \figreffig:comparison(c), we propose to feed the intermediate features from the head of the student to that of the teacher, yielding the cross-head predictions. Then, the KD operations can be conducted between the new cross-head predictions and the original predictions from the teacher. Despite its simplicity, CrossKD offers the following two main advantages. First, the KD loss does not affect the weight updating for part of the studentâ€™s head, avoiding the conflict between the original detection loss and the KD loss. In addition, since both the cross-head predictions and the teacherâ€™s predictions are produced by sharing part of the teacherâ€™s detection head, the cross-head predictions are relatively consistent with the teacherâ€™s predictions. This relieves the discrepancy between the teacher-student pair and enhances the training stability of prediction mimicking. Both advantages enable our CrossKD to efficiently distill knowledge from the teacherâ€™s predictions and hence result in even better performance than previous state-of-the-artÂ feature imitation methods.

Without bells and whistles, our method can significantly boost the performance of the student detector, achieving a faster training convergence. Comprehensive experiments on the COCO [33] dataset are conducted in this paper to elaborate the effectiveness of CrossKD. Specifically, with only prediction mimicking losses applied, CrossKD achieves 43.7 AP on GFL with 1Ã—\times training schedule, which is 3.5 AP higher than the baseline, surpassing all previous state-of-the-art object detection KD methods. Moreover, experiments also indicate our CrossKD is orthogonal to feature imitation methods. By combining CrossKD with the state-of-the-art feature imitation method, like PKDÂ [3], we further achieve 43.9 AP on GFL.

2 Related Work

2.1 Object Detection

Object detection is one of the most fundamental computer vision tasks, which requires recognizing and localizing objects simultaneously. Modern object detectors can be briefly divided into two categories: one-stageÂ [32, 49, 61, 29, 44] detectors and two-stageÂ [14, 13, 45, 31, 17, 50, 6, 16] detectors. Among them, one-stage detectors, also known as dense detectors, have emerged as the mainstream trend in detection due to their excellent speed-accuracy trade-off.

Dense object detectors have received great attention since YOLOv1Â [42]. Typically, YOLO series detectorsÂ [42, 43, 44, 2, 37, 12] attempt to balance the model size and their accuracy to meet the requirement of real-world applications. Anchor-free detectorsÂ [49, 22, 66] attempt to discard the design of anchor boxes to avoid time-consuming box operations and cumbersome hyper-parameter tuning. Dynamic label assignment methodsÂ [61, 40, 11] are proposed to better define the positive and negative samples for model learning. GFLÂ [29, 28] introduces Quality Focal Loss (QFL) and a Distribution-Guided Quality Predictor to increase the consistency between the classification score and the localization quality. It also models the bounding box representation as a probability distribution so that it can capture the localization ambiguity of the box edges. Recently, attributing to the strong ability of the transformer block to encode expressive features, DETR familyÂ [67, 4, 38, 24, 35, 59] have become a new trend in the object detection community.

2.2 Knowledge Distillation for Object Detection

Knowledge Distillation (KD) is an effective technique to transfer knowledge from a large-scale teacher model to a small-scale student model. It has been widely studied in the classification taskÂ [63, 8, 39, 54, 62, 47, 19, 21, 41, 58, 30], but it is still challenging to distill detection models because of the extreme background ratio. Pioneer work [5] proposes the first distillation framework for object detection by simply combining feature imitation and prediction mimicking. Since then, feature imitation has attracted more and more research attention as it shows more powerful distillation efficiency than prediction mimicking. Typically, some worksÂ [27, 52, 9] focus on selecting effective distillation regions for better feature imitation, while other works, like [15, 65, 25], aim to weight the imitation loss better. There are also some methodsÂ [57, 3, 56, 60] attempting to design new teacher-student consistency function, which aims to explore more consistency information or release the strict limit of Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss. Typically, FGDÂ [56] aligns the attention place for the teacher-student pair, while PKDÂ [3] maximizes the Pearson Correlation Coefficient between the feature representations.

As the earliest distillation strategy proposed inÂ [20], prediction mimicking plays a vital role in classification distillation. Recently, some improved prediction mimicking methods have been proposed to adapt to object detection. For example, Rank MimickingÂ [25] regards the score rank of the teacher as a kind of knowledge and aims to force the student to rank instances as the teacher. LDÂ [64] proposes to distill the localization distribution of bounding boxÂ [29] to transfer localization knowledge. Although LD shows the superiority of prediction mimicking to L2subscriptğ¿2L_{2} distance-based feature imitation, its accuracy gains are only comparable with the correlation-based feature imitation methods, e.g., PKDÂ [3], which pushes us to further explore the potential of prediction mimicking.

In this paper, we construct a CrossKD pipeline which separates detection and distillation into different heads to alleviate the target conflict problem of prediction mimicking. Itâ€™s worth noting that the cross-head strategyÂ [23, 1, 26, 55, 34] has been studied in classification tasks, which usually tend to design a complex pipeline. In object detection, HEADÂ [51] attempts the cross-head strategy, which delivers the student features to a newly built assistant head to bridge the gap between heterogeneous teacher-student pairs. In contrast, we observe that simply delivering the student feature to the teacher is effective enough to achieve SOTA results. This makes our method quite concise and different from the previous works mentioned above.

4 Experiments

4.1 Implement Details

We evaluate the proposed method on the large-scale MS COCOÂ [33] benchmark as done in most previous works. To ensure consistency with the standard practice, we use the trainval135k set (115K images) for training and the minival set (5K images) for validation. For evaluation, the standard COCO-style measurement, i.e., Average Precision (AP), is used. We also report mAP with IoU thresholds of 0.5 and 0.75, as well as AP for small, medium, and large objects. Our proposed method, CrossKD, is implemented under the MMDetectionÂ [7] framework in Python. For a fair comparison, all experiments are developed using 8 Nvidia V100 GPUs with a minibatch of two images per GPU. Unless otherwise stated, all the hyper-parameters follow the default settings of the corresponding student model for both training and testing.

4.2 Method Analysis

To investigate the effectiveness of our method, we conduct extensive ablation experiments based on GFLÂ [29]. If not specified, we use GFL with the ResNet-50 backboneÂ [18] as the teacher detector and use the ResNet-18 backbone in the student detector. The accuracy of the teacher and the student are 40.2 AP and 35.8 AP, respectively. All experiments follow the default 1Ã—\times training schedule (12 epochs).

Positions to apply CrossKD. As described in \secrefsubsec:method, CrossKD delivers the iğ‘–i-th intermediate feature of the student to part of the teacherâ€™s head. Here, we conduct distillation on both classification and box regression branches. When i=0ğ‘–0i=0, CrossKD directly feeds the studentâ€™s FPN features into the teacherâ€™s head. In this case, the entire studentâ€™s head is only supervised by the detection loss, and no distillation loss is involved. When i=nğ‘–ğ‘›i=n, our method degrades to the original prediction mimicking, where the distillation loss will be directly performed between the two predictions of the teacher-student pair.

CrossKD v.s. HEAD. HEADÂ [51] aims to distill knowledge between heterogeneous detectors. To bridge the gap between the teacher-student pair, HEAD delivers the student features to an assistant head with the same structure as the teacher. The assistant head is optimized during training by mimicking the features of the teacherâ€™s head. Despite similar adapting cross-head manner, CrossKD has relatively large differences from HEAD in the aspect of motivation and pipeline. Our CrossKD aims to accelerate the performance of prediction mimicking, which has been underestimated in previous work. During the investigation, we obverse that prediction mimicking suffers from the target conflict problem. Therefore, We propose CrossKD to separate the distillation manner from the student branch, which directly passes the student features to the teacherâ€™s head.

To further investigate those two strategies, we quantitatively compare CrossKD and HEAD and report the results inÂ \tabreftab:head. 5 Conclusions and Discussions

In this paper, we introduce CrossKD, a novel KD method designed to enhance the performance of dense object detectors. CrossKD transfers the intermediate features from the studentâ€™s head to that of the teacher to produce the cross-head predictions for distillation, an efficient way to alleviate the conflict between the supervised and distillation targets. Our results have shown that CrossKD can improve the distillation efficiency and achieve state-of-the-artÂ performance. We hope our work can provide the community with new insights. In the future, we will further extend our method to the multi-stage detectors (RCNN series), end-to-end detectors (DETR series), and other relevant fields, \eg3D object detection and instance segmentation.

6 Relation to Previous Works

In this section, we describe the differences of our method and some works related to this one, which are originally designed for the classification taskÂ [23, 1, 26, 55, 34]. Here, we compare CrossKD with these works from the aspects of motivation and structure to emphasize the differences.

Motivation. Previous works all concentrate on the classification task. For instance, Bai et al.Â [1] aims to alleviate overfitting in few-shot task. Li et al.Â [26] focuses on using a residual network to help a non-residual network overcome gradient vanishing. Some worksÂ [34, 55] target on the general KD scenario in classification. These methods all attempts to solve specific problems in classification and are not specially designed for distilling object detectors.

In contrast, CrossKD, which is specially designed for the object detection task, focuses on the target conflict problem in object detection. To our knowledge, this is the first work to discuss the target conflict problem in distilling object detectors. As presented in Sec. 1 of the main paper, the teacher detector usually predicts inaccurate results, which conflict with the ground-truth targets. The traditional KD methods supervise the student detector with those two controversial labels at the same place, resulting in low distillation efficiency. To alleviate this problem, we propose to deliver the intermediate features of the student to the part of the teacherâ€™s detection head and generate new cross-head predictions to accept the distillation losses.

Structure. Previous works tend to design a complicated manner to utilize the teacher-student latent features. Typically, Li et al.Â [26] forwards every stage features of the student into the teacherâ€™s blocks. Liu et al.Â [34] alternately delivers the intermediate features from the student to the teacher or from the teacher to the student. These strategies significantly increase the computational complexity in training phase.

Instead of applying a complicated design, CrossKD is relatively simple, which only passes the studentâ€™s latent features through part of the teacherâ€™s detection head. Despite its simplicity, extensive experiments demonstrate its effectiveness in object detection KD.

7 More Ablations

We also experiment with different cross-head strategies, which are illustrated inÂ \figreffig:others. As presented inÂ \tabreftab:cross-head, strategy (b), which differently reuses the studentâ€™s detection head, achieved only 35.4 AP, significantly lower than the 38.7 AP obtained by CrossKD. We hypothesize that this difference in performance may be attributed to the suboptimal optimization of the studentâ€™s blocks in this approach. \figreffig:others(c) and \figreffig:others(d) minimize the distances between the original predictions and the cross-head predictions. However, these strategies have limited impact on the studentâ€™s backbones, resulting in 34.5 AP and 32.5 AP for \figreffig:others(c) and \figreffig:others(d), respectively.

In contrast, CrossKD separates the distillation losses onto the teacherâ€™s branch and hence avoids the target conflict problem. As a result, CrossKD receives the highest AP of 38.7 among all cross-head strategies.
