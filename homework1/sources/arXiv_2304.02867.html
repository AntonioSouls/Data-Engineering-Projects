Abstract

Efficient point cloud representation is a fundamental element of Lidar-based 3D object detection. Recent grid-based detectors usually divide point clouds into voxels or pillars and construct single-stream networks in Bird’s Eye View. However, these point cloud encoding paradigms underestimate the point representation in the vertical direction, which cause the loss of semantic or fine-grained information, especially for vertical sensitive objects like pedestrian and cyclists. In this paper, we propose an explicit vertical multi-scale representation learning framework, VPFusion, to combine the complementary information from both voxel and pillar streams. Specifically, VPFusion first builds upon a sparse voxel-pillar-based backbone. The backbone divides point clouds into voxels and pillars, then encodes features with 3D and 2D sparse convolution simultaneously. Next, we introduce the Sparse Fusion Layer (SFL), which establishes a bidirectional pathway for sparse voxel and pillar features to enable the interaction between them. Additionally, we present the Dense Fusion Neck (DFN) to effectively combine the dense feature maps from voxel and pillar branches with multi-scale. Extensive experiments on the large-scale Waymo Open Dataset and nuScenes Dataset demonstrate that VPFusion surpasses the single-stream baselines by a large margin and achieves state-of-the-art performance with real-time inference speed.

1 Introduction

LiDAR-based 3D object detection methods have been widely adopted in autonomous driving and robot navigation systems, as point clouds from LiDAR sensors explicitly reflect geometric information and are rarely affected by weather conditions. However, unlike 2D images, point clouds are sparse and non-uniformly distributed, which brings two challenges for 3D object detection: Constructing efficient detection network and establishing robust object representation from points with divergent distributions.

There are several paradigms for point cloud representation. Among which, grid-based methods [60, 23, 21, 45, 22] quantize the point cloud into regular voxels or pillars and generate bounding boxes in 2D Bird’s Eye View (BEV), enables the usage of mature convolution architecture [30, 31]. Though superior to Range-view detectors [36, 2, 15] or point-based methods [42, 47, 57, 62] in both performance and efficiency, we argue that existing grid-based methods are insufficient at vertical point cloud representation learning, causing the loss of semantic or fine-grained information. Specifically, for detectors deploy voxels, commonly used 3D sparse submanifold convolution [19] restricts the feature diffusion from non-empty voxel to empty voxel, which leads to the limited receptive field in the vertical direction. On the contrary, pillar-based methods discretized point clouds into vertical volumes, where a pillar encodes all neighboring points in the specific X-Y coordinate. This paradigm obtains the full-ranged vertical receptive field. However, it is weak in capturing fine-grained features.

To analyze the impact of vertical representation on the grid-based methods, we conduct an exploratory experiment on Waymo Open Dataset [50]. We first split the ground truth (GT) according to its vertical point distribution: Given each GT box, we uniformly divide it into 101010 bins in the vertical direction, then calculate the vertical sparsity as SZ=Nb​i​n/10subscript𝑆𝑍subscript𝑁𝑏𝑖𝑛10S_{Z}=N_{bin}/10, where Nb​i​nsubscript𝑁𝑏𝑖𝑛N_{bin} denotes the number of bins which contains at least 111 point. Next, we count the recall VS. vertical sparsity curve of two representative grid-based detectors [60, 21] at the specific horizontal occupancy or point density. The horizontal occupancy is defined as the geometric mean of the sparsities in the X𝑋X and Y𝑌Y axes: SX​SYsubscript𝑆𝑋subscript𝑆𝑌\sqrt{S_{X}S_{Y}}. As illustrated in Fig. 1, Voxel-based CenterPoint_IoU [60, 23]) is better when the vertical sparsity is high, while pillar-based PillarNet [21] is superior in the sparse vertical distribution. This phenomenon proves the insufficient vertical representation in the voxel or pillar-based methods, while the complementary performance under the variant vertical sparsities encourages us to combine the voxels and pillars for vertical encoding enhancement.

In this paper, we propose Voxel-Pillar Fusion (VPFusion), a single-stage two-stream 3D detector that can capture complementary vertical information from both voxels and pillars. We first design a sparse voxel-pillar-based backbone, which divides the point clouds into voxels and pillars, then encodes sparse volume features with 3D and 2D sparse convolutions simultaneously. To enlarge the vertical receptive field of voxels and enrich the fine-grained information in pillars, we present the Sparse Fusion Layer (SFL) to establish the voxel-pillar bidirectional connection. To be specific, SFL aggregates voxel features in the vertical direction and broadcasts pillar features to corresponding vertical voxels, then fuses the aggregated and broadcasted features with the original pillar and voxel features. Moreover, we present the Dense Fusion Neck (DFN) to combine the multi-scale dense feature maps from voxel and pillar branches. We conduct extensive experiments on nuScenes and Waymo Open Dataset. The results indicate that VPFusion achieves state-of-the-art performance with real-time inference speed, and indeed supplements the vertical representation from both voxels and pillars as demonstrated in Fig. 1.

Our contributions are summarized as follows:

  • •

    We point out that existing grid-based methods are insufficient for vertical representation learning, and propose a two-stream 3D detector that captures complementary information from both voxels and pillars.

  • •

    We propose the Sparse Fusion Layer, which enlarges the vertical receptive field of voxels and enriches the fine-grained information in pillars, and further present the Dense Fusion Neck to fuse the multi-scale dense feature maps from voxel and pillar branches.

  • •

    We conduct thorough experiments on the large-scale datasets to validate the necessity and feasibility of the voxel-pillar fusion, and also analyze the inference speed of VPFusion with different model complexity.

2 Related Work

2.1 Grid-based 3D Object Detection

Detectors Encoded via Voxels. Voxel-based methods divide the point clouds into 3D voxels and deploy 3D convolutions for voxel-wise feature extraction. Pioneering work VoxelNet [67] replaces the hand-crafted representation with the proposed voxel feature encoding layer, enabling the end-to-end training procedure. Then, SECOND [55] introduce the 3D sparse convolution [18, 19] to avoid the redundant computation on empty voxels. Two-stage detectors [33, 10, 60] follow the coarse-to-fine pattern in 2D detection [43, 30], which generate Region of Interests (RoIs) in the first stage and refine the predictions with specific RoI features. For instance, Voxel R-CNN [10] proposes the voxel RoI pooling to extract RoI features from the voxels. As a contrast, single-stage methods [63, 64, 23] directly generate bounding boxes in one stage. CIA-SSD [63] presents the IoU-aware confidence rectification to make the confidence consistent with regression accuracy. AFDetV2 [23] proposes a multi-task framework to incorporate keypoint auxiliary supervision with IoU-aware prediction, which achieves competitive results. More recently, some works [34, 44, 35, 22] explore the powerful transformer architecture [53, 12, 24, 32] in 3D object detection. For example, VoxSeT [22] presents a voxel-based set attention module to model the long-range dependencies in voxelized point clusters. To the best of our knowledge, the limitation of vertical representation learning in voxel-based methods has not been studied.

Pillar-based 3D Object Detection. Pillar-based detectors encode the point clouds in Bird’s Eye View, which directly produce the 2D features, thus improving the computation efficiency. PIXOR [56] represents the scene from BEV space, facilitating the application of 2D convolution for object prediction. Then, PointPillars [26] convert the point clouds to 2D pillars (vertical volumes) and deploys PointNet [40, 41] as the encoder to generate pillar features. The following work HVNet [59] presents the hybrid voxel feature encoding strategy to aggregate multi-scale 2D voxels into unified point-wise features. PillarNet [21] proposes the 2D sparse convolution-based backbone for efficient pillar encoding. It achieves promising performance while running at real-time speed. Finally, SST [13] analyzes the characteristic of 3D objects in BEV, then proposes a single-stride transformer to boost the performance on the challenging pedestrian category. As mentioned in Sec. 1, pillar-based representation brings ambiguity in the vertical direction.

2.2 Multi-Source Feature Fusion

Methods of multi-source fusion are proposed to combine complementary information from different sources. For instance, point-voxel join representation [58, 48, 45, 38] is presented to integrate the flexible receptive field with efficient feature learning schemes. STD [58] generates proposals from point-wise features and deploys the PointPool layer to provide dense voxel features for box refinement. In contrast, PV-RCNN [45] regresses the proposals from a 3D sparse convolution-based encoder, then aggregates voxel features into keypoints via proposed voxel set abstraction for box refinement. Additionally, multi-view fusion [6, 25, 66, 54] is another paradigm for information supplementation. MVF [66] utilizes the complementary information from both BEV and perspective view, while Pillar-OD [54] applies pillar encoder in BEV and cylindrical view. Recently, multi-modal fusion [29, 39, 1, 27] has achieved remarkable progress. CLOCS [39] proposes a late fusion strategy to exploit the geometric and semantic consistencies between 2D and 3D predictions. DeepFusion [27] presents InverseAug and LearnableAlign to align multi-modal features in the late stage. While multi-source fusion provides extra information from different sources, it also raises a problem. As the separate sources usually vary, effective alignment of multi-source features becomes challenging and sophisticated. In our work, we design a simple yet efficient structure for voxel-pillar fusion.

3 Methodology

3.1 Overview

Motivated by enhancing the point cloud vertical representation, we propose Voxel-Pillar Fusion (VPFusion), a single-stage two-stream 3D object detector to capture complementary information from 2D and 3D volumes. As shown in Fig. 2, VPFusion first quantizes the point clouds into voxels and pillars with the same resolution in the X-Y plane. Then, we adopt the Sparse Voxel-Pillar-based Backbone (Sec. 3.2). It has four stages, each stage is composed of a sparse conv block, where 3D and 2D sparse convolutions form two branches for sparse voxel and pillar feature extraction. To enrich the local context for the pillar branch and introduce the vertical semantic information to the voxel branch, VPFusion deploys the Sparse Fusion Layer (SFL) at the end of each stage, which establishes a bidirectional pathway between the sparse voxel and pillar features (Sec. 3.3). Next, Dense Fusion Neck (Sec. 3.4) fuses multi-scale sparse features from different branches and generates the dense feature maps in Bird’s Eye View (BEV). Finally, we apply the common center-based detection head [60] with IoU-Aware rectification function [23] for object prediction (Sec. 3.5).

Consistent Voxel-Pillar Encoder. Voxels and pillars are two different point cloud representations. However, with the same resolution in the X-Y plane, the vertical collection of voxels contains the same point clouds with the corresponding pillar under the specific X-Y coordinates. Sparse Conv Block. We adopt the two-stream sparse conv blocks to extract sparse voxel and pillar features. The convolution block is empowered by both 3D and 2D sparse convolutions. Similar to conventional framework [55, 21], for each block, the voxel branch consists of a 3D regular sparse convolution and several 3D submanifold sparse convolutional layers, while the pillar branch is composed of 2D counterpart likewise. The regular sparse convolutions are applied for spatial downsampling, and submanifold sparse convolutions constrain the output locations identical to the input location, which maintains efficiency. As depicted in Fig. 3, we set the kernel size, stride, padding operation to be equal for 2D and 3D regular sparse convolutions in the horizontal dimension (L𝐿L and W𝑊W dimension), because it ensures voxel and pillar features dilate to the same location in the X-Y plane. In such manner, non-empty voxels and pillars possess consistent occupancy in BEV space, i.e., each non-empty pillar has several corresponding non-empty voxels (≥1absent1\geq 1) at the same X-Y coordinates. This consistency facilitates the fusion of sparse voxel and pillar features.

Overall, the sparse voxel-pillar-based backbone consists of 4 stages, where sparse conv blocks are deployed to sequentially generate sparse voxel and pillar features with 1×,2×,4×,1\times,~{}2\times,~{}4\times, and 8×8\times downsampled sizes. Then, the sparse pillar features are directly transformed into the dense BEV feature map, while voxel features are stacked along the Z𝑍Z axis after they transfer to the dense features.

3.4 Dense Fusion Neck

After the sparse backbone, sparse features are converted to dense BEV feature maps. The dense features are processed by the 2D dense CNN for receptive field enrichment and center-positioned object feature diffusion [60, 21]. In our framework, to fully exploit the dense feature maps from both voxel and pillar branches, we design the Dense Fusion Neck (DFN). DFN follows the common hierarchical structure [30, 55] for multi-scale feature aggregation. As shown in Fig. 2, we apply convolution blocks B​l​o​c​k​(M,D)𝐵𝑙𝑜𝑐𝑘𝑀𝐷Block(M,D) to extract dense features with 8×8\times and 16×16\times downsampled sizes in voxel/pillar branch separately, where B​l​o​c​k​(M,D)𝐵𝑙𝑜𝑐𝑘𝑀𝐷Block(M,D) represents M𝑀M convolution layers with D𝐷D output dimension. Next, dense voxel and pillar features with the same scale are fused by element-wise summation. Afterward, we combine the different scale features via upsampling and concatenation, then aggregate the final features by another convolution block. Note that 8×8\times downsampled voxel and pillar features are processed discriminatively. As mentioned in Sec. 3.2, to obtain 2D BEV maps, voxel features are concatenated along the Z𝑍Z axis. Consequently, the feature semantics are divided into several groups along the channel dimension, causing the mismatch with pillar features. Based on this, we apply B​l​o​c​k​(M,D)𝐵𝑙𝑜𝑐𝑘𝑀𝐷Block(M,D) to the voxel branch for feature semantic transformation, while 8×8\times downsampled pillar features are processed by a single conv layer for channel conversion.

4 Experiments

4.1 Datasets

Waymo Open Dataset. Waymo Open Dataset (WOD) [50] consists of 798 training, 202 validation, and 150 testing sequences with 200K annotated frames. The detection range covers the area of [−75​m,−75​m,75​m,75​m]75𝑚75𝑚75𝑚75𝑚[-75m,-75m,75m,75m], and evaluation metrics are average precision (AP) and average precision weighted by heading (APH). Test samples are split into two difficulties, LEVEL 1 for objects with more than 5 inside points and LEVEL 2 for objects with at least 1 point.

nuScenes Dataset. The nuScenes dataset [3] contains 700 training, 150 validation, and 150 testing sequences, with 34K annotated keyframes and 10 categories. The evaluation metric is nuScenes detection score (NDS), which is a weighted sum of mAP and other true positive metrics.

5 Conclusion

In this paper, we propose VPFusion, a single-stage two-stream 3D detector for robust vertical point cloud representation. VPFusion adpots the sprase voxel-pillar-based backbone for consistent voxel-pillar encoding, and applies the Sparse Fusion Layer and Dense Fusion Neck to efficiently capture complementary information from voxel and pillar branches, resulting in state-of-the-art performance on the Waymo Open Dataset with real-time inference speed.

Limitations. VPFusion relies on consistent occupancy in the bird’s eye view for element-wise fusion, which restricts the voxel and pillar partition to be equal horizontally. For future work, we will explore the asynchronous voxel-pillar fusion strategies (e.g., variant resolution or multi-stride fusion) for more flexible point cloud representation learning.
