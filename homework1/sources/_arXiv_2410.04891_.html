Abstract

Recent personalization methods for diffusion models, such as Dreambooth, allow fine-tuning pre-trained models to generate new concepts. However, applying these techniques across multiple tasks in order to include, e.g., several new objects or styles, leads to mutual interference between their adapters. While recent studies attempt to mitigate this issue by combining trained adapters across tasks after fine-tuning, we adopt a more rigorous regime and investigate the personalization of large diffusion models under a continual learning scenario, where such interference leads to catastrophic forgetting of previous knowledge. To that end, we evaluate the naÃ¯ve continual fine-tuning of customized models and compare this approach with three methods for consecutive adaptersâ€™ training: sequentially merging new adapters, merging orthogonally initialized adapters, and updating only relevant parameters according to the task. In our experiments, we show that the proposed approaches mitigate forgetting when compared to the naÃ¯ve approach.

1 Introduction

Deep Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs)Â [28] have revolutionized image generation, with state-of-the-art performance in synthesizing highly detailed objects and blending them with complex styles. Moreover, personalization techniques such as DreamboothÂ [24], combined with the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)Â [12], introduce the possibility to easily customize large-scale pre-trained models. In those techniques, a selected subset of model parameters is updated with just a few training examples to allow for the generation of objects not present in the original training set.

Unfortunately, continuous customization of a generative model over several tasks leads to the catastrophic forgettingÂ [6] of previously encoded knowledge due to mutual interference between tasksâ€™ adapters. Recent studies try to mitigate this issue through additional regularizationÂ [27] or by careful merging of the trained adaptersÂ [26, 7]. For such merging to succeed, all adapters have to be available at the same time to properly scaleÂ [26] or selectÂ [18] the resulting modelsâ€™ weights. In practice, with a bigger number of tasks, this assumption is often unfeasible, as storing numerous copies of model weights, even in the form of a sparse low-rank adaptation matrix, is memory-consuming (e.g. approximately 8 LoRA weight matrices with rank 64646464 are equal to the size of all adapted parameters in Stable Diffusion XLâ€™sÂ [21] model). Therefore, in this work, we propose to study the effectiveness of merging techniques under the strict continual learning regime where only the model or model with a single adapter are passed between tasks.

In a series of experiments, we evaluate (1) NaÃ¯ve continual fine-tuning of the low-rank adapter with three merging methods mitigating forgetting of concepts and styles: (2) consecutive merging of task-specific adapters, (3) merging LoRAs initialized with orthogonal weights, and (4) merging through a selection of weights with the highest magnitude for the taskÂ [18]. Our experiments indicate that adding multiple adapters in a NaÃ¯ve way can lead to a situation where a model converges towards its base form, while all the evaluated techniques mitigate this issue.

2 Related work

Diffusion models personalization and customization

The topic of Diffusion Models (DMs) personalization has been broadly studied in recent years. Several works, including Textual InversionÂ [8] learn new word embeddings to represent custom and unique concepts. This idea is further extended by several approachesÂ [17, 16], with additional regularizationÂ [33], or features mappingÂ [32]. On the other hand, DreamBoothÂ [24] fine-tunes the entire model to incorporate specific subjects into generated images. This idea is further extended in HyperDreamBoothÂ [25] where a hypernetworkÂ [10] is used to generate personalized weights. Recent personalization techniques employ Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)Â [12] to reduce the computational requirements by fine-tuning small, low-rank matrices and keeping the base model frozen. Nevertheless, simply using LoRA does not prevent interference. Therefore, ZipLoRAÂ [26] optimizes separately trained adapters for style and object to simplify their merging, and B-LoRAÂ [7] targets different attention layers of diffusion models for both style and object adapters. On top of those, weights2weightsÂ [5] introduces a personalized adapter space, enabling the creation of entirely new personalized models. OFTÂ [22] and Orthogonal AdaptationÂ [20] leverage orthogonalization to merge models seamlessly without compromising fidelity.

Generating style with diffusion models

Apart from introducing new objects to the model, there is a line of research aiming for style generation and editing in DDPMs. StyleDropÂ [29] leverages the Muse transformer to fine-tune text-to-image models for generating style-specific images. StyleAlignedÂ [11] employs shared attention to maintain a consistent style across generated images, while Style SimilarityÂ [30] provides a framework for style retrieval from diffusion modelsâ€™ training data and introduces CSD, an image encoder that accurately captures the style of images. UnlearnCanvasÂ [36] offers a comprehensive dataset for evaluating machine unlearning in stylized image generation. For knowledge localization, Diff-QuickFixÂ [2] and LOCOEDITÂ [1] enable precise editing of diffusion modelsâ€™ knowledge of specific styles by identifying style-relevant layers.

Continual Learning of Diffusion Models

There are works using DMs in CL scenarios. InÂ [35], the authors explore if contemporary CL methods prevent forgetting in diffusion models. InÂ [9], DDPM is used as a source of rehearsal examples, whileÂ [4] extends this idea with classifier guidance towards easy-to-forget examples. Similarly, inÂ [15], authors use an externally trained text-to-image diffusion model for the same purpose. To mitigate artifacts introduced with generative replay, especially with low-rank adaptation, inÂ [27], the authors introduce C-LoRA, where consecutive tasks are learned with a self-regularization technique that penalizes updates corresponding to already altered weights.

Model merging

The concept of editing models through arithmetic operations applied on modelsâ€™ weights is explored in [13], where authors describe mathematical operations between task vectors, defined as the difference between the weights of the fine-tuned and the base model. To limit the interference between tasks, recent methods replace straightforward averaging with summation of trimmed parametersÂ [34], Fisher-information-based mergingÂ [19] or with guidance from discriminative modelÂ [14]. In this work, we adapt the MagMax methodÂ [18] that is used to continually train the CLIP model by selecting, from several task vectors, weights with the highest magnitude.

4 Experiments

Experimental setup

In our experiments, we use the DreamBoothÂ [24] dataset for fine-tuning on objects, and UnlearnCanvasÂ [36] for styles. We perform fine-tuning on the 10101010 consecutive tasks and average results over 4 random tasks ordering, with 2 random seeds each. To measure the performance of the model, we use cosine similarity to the reference image with DINOÂ [3] and CLIPÂ [23] feature extractors for object alignment measure and DINO and CSDÂ [31] for style similarity. We report the performance of the final model fine-tuned over all 10 tasks with the Average Score and Average Forgetting metrics. More details about the experimental setup are provided in the Appendix A.

5 Conclusions

In this work, we analyze the continual adaptation capabilities of a DreamBooth method coupled with the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) technique. We evaluate three different approaches to merging and initialization of the individual adapters. We show that naÃ¯ve continual training of a LoRA leads to catastrophic forgetting, while other techniques can mitigate this issue, which originates from conflicts in adapters that are sequentially trained.
