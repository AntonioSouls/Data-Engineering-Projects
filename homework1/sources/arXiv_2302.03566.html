Look around and learn: self-improving object detection by exploration

Gianluca Scarpellini ‚ÄÉ‚ÄÉ Stefano Rosa ‚ÄÉ‚ÄÉ Pietro Morerio ‚ÄÉ‚ÄÉ Lorenzo Natale ‚ÄÉ‚ÄÉ Alessio Del Bue
Abstract

Object detectors often experience a drop in performance when new environmental conditions are insufficiently represented in the training data. This paper studies how to automatically fine-tune a pre-existing object detector while exploring and acquiring images in a new environment without relying on human intervention, i.e., in an utterly self-supervised fashion. In our setting, an agent initially learns to explore the environment using a pre-trained off-the-shelf detector to locate objects and associate pseudo-labels. By assuming that pseudo-labels for the same object must be consistent across different views, we learn an exploration policy mining hard samples and we devise a novel mechanism for producing refined predictions from the consensus among observations. Our approach outperforms the current state-of-the-art, and it closes the performance gap against a fully supervised setting without relying on ground-truth annotations. We also compare various exploration policies for the agent to gather more informative observations. Code and dataset will be made available upon paper acceptance

Keywords:
Action-Perception; active exploration; self-training

1 Introduction

When newborns interact with novel objects, they are naturally inclined to actively explore their hidden facets by manipulation or by moving around to have views from a new angle, thus unveiling blindsides. Yet, a child is aware that, no matter the viewpoint, the object is always the same. Findings in social science suggest that humans and animals develop perception and learn by actively exploring an environment [14] in a so called action-perception loop [25], with very little or no external supervision. Inspired by human perception, in this work we posit that the process of unsupervised fine-tuning of object detectors can indeed benefit from an active exploration of the environment (Figure 1). Such benefit can be further amplified if the agent can actively learn how to choose the views it experiences, in order to make learning more efficient.

We suggest that mining training samples in the environment by seeing the same object instances from different views may improve the agent‚Äôs object detection skills. For this reason, we introduce a learnable policy that is indeed oriented to the ultimate goal of improving object detection, without external supervision, by directly rewarding the acquisition of uncertain samples. Our proposal extends previous literature on environment exploration, which mostly leverages reinforcement learning as a way of maximizing the total area explored in the process [7], the novelty of the observation [29], or success in reaching a predefined goal [45, 10].

A perception module (object detector) driven by active exploration is somehow in contrast with the most classic paradigm of computer vision, where models are usually trained with static datasets and do not fully benefit from the realistic continuity of the 3D world. Our perception module adopts its predictions as pseudo-labels to improve the performances with no external supervision. Although self-supervised learning with multiple ‚Äúviews‚Äù of the same image has been recently gaining popularity, such views are usually obtained with a limited set of augmentations ([18, 17, 11]), which cannot fully capture the complexity of a 3D object seen by different viewpoints.

The proposed pipeline includes learning both action and perception stages in a completely self-supervised fashion (see Figure 2) and it assumes an agent exploring an unknown environment using an off-the-shelf object detector (MaskRCNN [19] in our experiments) to detect objects in the scene. Action: we design and train a reinforcement learning policy with the objective of maximizing the utility of the observations collected by the agent. We hypothesize that the utility of a pseudo-label for an object is higher if that object is predicted as belonging to different classes when seen from different points of views. Building upon this intuition, we propose a score to measure predictions disagreement as the average distance of the normalized logits, and we reward the policy if it maximizes the disagreement score, which is progressively gathered in a disagreement map. During the exploration phase, a 3D semantic voxel map is progressively built, which contains all the predictions gathered during exploration. Such predictions are extremely noisy, since the detector is far from perfect and images from the new scenario are likely to be visually different from the training ones. Also, the same object instance are predicted as belonging to different classes when seen from different points of view, or even not detected at all in some images. Perception: The predictions gathered during exploration are then used as pseudo-labels to finetune the detector, in a three-fold manner. First, we enforce the set of predictions to be consistent across multiple views via a consensus mechanism. The refined pseudo-labels are assigned to all corresponding images. This consensus is used as a feedback to fine-tune the detector. Second, no matter the assigned pseudo-label, we force the model backbone to map different views of the same object close in the feature space while views of different instances are pushed afar, in a contrastive learning fashion. Third, we exploit the soft targets obtained by averaging the multiple prediction acquired for each instance as additional feedback, to fully exploit the multiple acquired views.

In summary, the contributions of this work are the following:

  • ‚Ä¢

    We revisit the Action-Perception paradigm to tightly couple action and perception stages. Action and perception work synergically with the ultimate goal of improving the object detector;

  • ‚Ä¢

    A novel disagreement map and reward for guiding the action phase. The action policy learns how to gather observations which maximize disagreement, as a way of mining hard training sample for the perception step;

  • ‚Ä¢

    A self-training perception module specifically designed to leverage the inconsistent, noisy and contrasting pseudo-labels gathered during exploration;

  • ‚Ä¢

    A comparison between different action policies and perception modules.

    Our experiments show that finding hard examples to feed the detector leads to more informative pseudo-labels resulting in more accurate object detectors.

2 Related work

This paper aims at improving computer-vision models, such as object detectors, by leveraging an embodied agent that actively explores an area. Object detectors are indeed a key component for goal-oriented exploration [8, 5, 7] and recent literature showed that object detection improvements lead to reliable semantic mapping of the environment [8], with useful implications for robotics exploration. The discussion proceeds as follows. This section first highlights recent literature in pseudo-labeling and self-training. Next, we focus on object detection for active perception and visual exploration.
Pseudo-labeling in object detection. Pseudo-labeling is a fundamental technique in self-supervised learning. The approach is commonly defined as adopting predictions from a neural-network as labels from unlabeled data [22]. Despite its effectiveness, simply providing neural-network outputs commonly suffer from confirmation bias‚Äînetwork confidence improves even wrong predictions‚Äîand high class imbalance [1]. Similar behaviors are commonly observed in object detectors, where pseudo-labels are hard to exploit due to high foreground-background imbalance [26]. Inspired by previous works [40, 39] on consistency training, we propose to solve for the confirmation bias by correcting the pseudo-labels through a consensus mechanism. In particular, our approach leverages the 3D information of the environment to produce pseudo-labels for every observations, even for observations where the model do not provide predictions. This technique can mitigate the imbalance issue by limiting the number of false negatives examples in training data.
Self-training. Our method shares similarities with the self-training paradigm. During self-training, a model is trained jointly on labeled and unlabeled data [22]. A pre-trained model is adopted to provide pseudo-labels for unlabeled data. Recent literature suggests to generate pseudo-labels with noise-free models and data while training with strong data augmentation and noise injection (e.g., dropout) [1, 41, 12]. Li et al. [23] adapted self-training via data augmentation to object detection by introducing a selective model to provide good positive-negative pairs for object detection. Nevertheless, their method adds overhead to the training, as the selective model must be trained jointly with the object detector. Chaplot et al. [6] proposed an approach for collecting consistent pseudo-labels in an environment. They build a semantic map of the environment and re-project it onto each frame for getting consistent pseudo-labels across views. Differently from them, we leverage the inconsistency for helping the policy during the exploration phase and provide this information to the object-detector.

We introduce instead a novel mechanism to leverage different points of view of the same object as a data-augmentation strategy. We adopt a contrastive loss [32] to impose that features for an object should be alike across views, while different objects should be distinguishable in the feature space. In this sense, we interpret our approach as a generalization of the cropping strategy, which is quite popular in self-supervised learning [11].
Object detection for active visual exploration. Our proposal shares its underlining principle with active perception, especially in the context of visual exploration of the environment. Active perception can represent a broad spectrum of concepts [3], but in the context of robotics it describes the problem of actively moving in an environment to collect useful examples and subsequently improve the performance on a given task.

Active perception has been extensively studied in the context of question and answering [16, 36] and goal-oriented exploration [8, 5, 7, 15]. More recently, there has been an interest in embodied perception for improving object detection. Yang et al. [44] propose to merge FasterRCNN with an actor-critic agent to control the viewing angle of a partially occluded object to improve its recognition, using a custom dataset extracted from a simulator. Chaplot et al. [9] proposed to learn exploration policies for online data collection. They introduced an intrinsic reward based on discrepancy between different detections projected to the ground plane. This does not fully address the problem of objects overlapping when projected from their 3D pose to the ground (e.g., a cup on a table). Chaplot et al. [6] train an exploration policy based on the number of confident objects predictions. Nevertheless, their approach does not consider the inconsistency of the detections as a viable training signal.

Recent works also proposed alternative solutions to the problem which rely only partially on semantic annotations [4, 27, 13]. These works rely on ground-truth annotations and do not consider self-supervised settings where labels are provided by a pre-trained model.

3 Methodology

We equip a simulated agent with an object-detector, a RGB-D sensor, and a position sensor. The agent looks around and collects samples from a set of simulated environments [38] in the Habitat simulator [35]. A policy sets the long-term goals for the agent, which moves toward the goal by choosing actions among moving forward, turn left, and turn right. We aim to fine-tune the agent‚Äôs object-detector by relying only on the samples it generates autonomously, i.e. without ground-truth annotations. As in previous literature [9, 6], we adopt the object-detector MaskRCNN [19] with a Resnet50 backbone [20]; the model is provided by [37] and is pre-trained on COCO dataset [24]. We now formalise and provide further details on the two stages of our methodology: the action phase and the perception phase (Figure 2).
Action. During the action phase, we train a policy œÄùúã\pi parametrised as a neural network to predict long-term goals. We propose the use of a disagreement reward to train the agent‚Äôs movement policy.

We first project the objects detected by the object detector into a semantic voxel-map by leveraging the agent‚Äôs RGB-D and position sensors. For each unique object instance, we compute a disagreement score as the cosine distance among the logits predictions for that object (Equation 1). We aggregate the disagreement scores into a 2D disagreement map, which we input into the policy, and we reward the agent for maximizing their sum. Our intuition is that, by maximizing this reward, the agent is encouraged to explore different points of view of each object and find inconsistencies among the resulting predictions. Next, a planner produces the set of optimal sub-goals to reach the final goal. The agent takes actions toward each intermediate goal for NreplanningsubscriptùëÅreplanningN_{\text{replanning}} steps before predicting a new long-term goal. Moreover, the agent may miss a detection if the object is hard to recognize (e.g., if it sees the object from the back). Both scenarios introduce inconsistencies in the training data that can compromise the model‚Äôs performance if directly adopted for self-training.

4 Experiments

We devise a set of experiments to test policies and fine-tuning techniques separately. For each policy described in Section 4.1, we let an agent move in the environment and collect 7,500 frames from the Gibson dataset [38]. We opt for MaskRCNN [19] as the object detector for being comparable with previous approaches, although our proposed approach is model-agnostic.

4.1 Action and perception methods

Action. We compare our action module with classic and learned policies.
Random goals ‚Äì implements a random policy based on long-term goals. The agent chooses a random goal among the possible goals. Next, the agent moves to the selected point via a global path planner that produces smooth trajectories.
Frontier exploration [43] ‚Äì implements classical frontier-based active exploration [43]. The agent keeps an internal representation of the explored map, computes goal points of interest at the edge of the explored map, selects the next best point according to its information gain (the amount of open space around the goal point), and moves toward the goal via a global path planner.
NeuralSLAM [7] ‚Äì reinforcement learning policy based on long-term and short-term goal prediction for maximising map coverage. A global policy predicts the next long-term goal, while a local policy predicts a sequence of short-term steps to move toward the final goal. A local path planner guides the agent toward each short-term step. The reward is the percentage of exploration of the environment.
Semantic Curiosity [9] ‚Äì RL exploration policy that predicts local steps to maximize inconsistency between detections projected onto the ground plane.
SEAL [6] ‚Äì reinforcement learning policy for maximising the number of confident predictions of the detector. The agent builds a voxel map of the environment by leveraging the sensor information. A global policy processes the current voxel map and position information and predicts the next long-term goal, while a local path-planner guides the agent toward the goal. The reward is the number of voxel assigned to any possible class with score above 0.90.90.9.
Ours ‚Äì our reinforcement learning policy, which maximizes the disagreement across different views of the objects of the scenes, as discussed in Section 3.1.

Perception. We compare our perception module with two baselines:
Self-training [42] ‚Äì the perception phase consists in adopting the prediction of the off-the-shelf object detector as pseudo-labels for training, without any further processing. We note that self-training often requires several millions of images to provide strong benefits.
SEAL perception [6]‚Äì SEAL aggregates the predictions of the off-the-shelf object-detector into a semantic voxel map by assigning to each voxel the class with maximum score among the predicted classes. Then, the voxel-map back is projected onto each observation. Finally, the detector is fine-tuned by minimizing the classical MaskRCNN losses [19].
Ours ‚Äì our perception module applies multi-view consistencies among predictions while retaining the inconsistency information as soft-target and imposing feature vectors of the same object to be close when the object is seen from different views, as discussed in Section 3.2.

To validate our method, we conduct experiments across different policies and different perception methods. Table 1 reports our results. As a reference, we assess that the off-the-shelf MaskRCNN [19] reaches mAP@50 40.3340.3340.33 on our test set. Our perception method outperforms self-training [42] and SEAL‚Äôs perception [6] in combination with random, frontier, and NeuralSLAM baselines. The improvement is evident when we combine our self-training mechanism and our policy: in this scenario, our action + our perception reaches the overall best result of mAP@50 45.6545.6545.65, which is 5.2%percent5.25.2\% over off-the-shelf performance. Even when using the random goals baseline, therefore without relying on any predetermined behavior, our perception module improves over self-training by 2.21%percent2.212.21\%. When compared with exploration policies, both learned [7] and classical [43] ones, our perception module outperforms SEAL perception. In particular, we notice that a classical frontier exploration baseline outperforms NeuralSLAM policy in our self-learning task. We believe NeuralSLAM exploration collects fewer observations for each object instances. In a similar scenario, where the variance of the observations is low, the semantic voxel map reflects the labels predicted by the off-the-shelf detector. In fact, we report similar results for all the perception methods when NeuralSLAM is adopted. Semantic Curiosity predicts only local actions, which limits the capacity of the policy to explore the environment and, therefore, the collection of training data. On the other hand, a frontier exploration baseline combined with our perception module achieves an AP of 43.0943.0943.09. Our intuition is that, due to the greedy exploration policy, the agent tends to move through the same areas multiple times, collecting more views of the same object, that are then cleaned by the consensus approach. SEAL perception strategy slightly outperforms our perception when both are applied in conjunction with SEAL policy. Intuitively, SEAL agent finds views of an object with the highest prediction score, which could lead to less variance, therefore vanishing the benefits of our consensus mechanism. Indeed we underline that our action-perception loop outperforms SEAL‚Äôs one by 2.64%percent2.642.64\%. We provide more in-depth analysis of the policies behaviors in the Supplementary Material.

When our policy is combined with self-training, our approach underperforms with respect to the other policies. We underline that this result is expected. Our policy, unlike others, explores the environment to purposely find conflicting observations. Therefore, off-the-shelf predictions contain disagreeing pseudo-labels, which leads to poor results. Both SEAL and our perception are able to solve the inconsistencies. Nonetheless, we show that our perception strategy fully exploits the inconsistencies and outperforms SEAL perception by 1.15%percent1.151.15\%.

We conduct an additional study by fine-tuning with ground truth labels, which can be interpreted as an upper-bound of our approach. Not surprisingly, the ground-truth labels outperform pseudo-labels for every policy. Nevertheless, our action+perception scores the highest performance with respect to supervised training, as it is only slightly worse than ground-truth.

5 Conclusion

In this work we proposed a novel self-training mechanism for object detection that relies on the active exploration of the environment. The object-detector benefits from hard positive examples, e.g. where the off-the-shelf detector fails. Namely, the perception phase can exploit disagreements among pseudo-labels. Building upon this intuition, our contribution covers the whole action-perception loop. From the action side, we design a reinforcement learning policy to maximize the number of disagreeing examples, i.e. when the object detector assigns different classes to the same object instance.

We propose to clean the inconsistencies by using a semantic voxel map by accumulating each prediction into the 3D space, and in particular we further inject the conflicting information among predictions into the training as consistent logits vector for each object. Finally, we force the feature vector of each object to be consistent across different views. Experiments show the benefits of combining the proposed action and perception stages which outperform the baselines and reaches state-of-the-art performance, improving the off-the-shelf object-detector mAP by 5.3%percent5.35.3\%.

In this section, we discuss qualitatively how our policy compares to the baselines. Figure 6 reports a trajectory sampled with each policy on an unseen scene (Pablo) of the Gibson dataset [38].
